## Purpose

This KB helps narrative practitioners design the actual research instruments they will use in the field — the questions they ask, the frameworks they observe with, the surveys they distribute. It is oriented specifically toward narrative listening: listening for how communities make sense of the world, what frames they use, what they make visible and invisible, and what language they reach for.

These instruments are designed by the agent but belong to the practitioner. They should always be reviewed, adjusted for cultural context, and tested before use. The practitioner knows their community — the agent provides a starting structure.

---

## What Narrative Listening Is Listening For

Standard research instruments listen for opinions, experiences, and facts. Narrative listening listens for something different — the cognitive architecture underneath the opinion:

- **Frames:** What does this person take for granted? What do they assume everyone already knows? What is so obvious to them it doesn't need to be said?
- **Metaphors:** How do they describe abstract things — the economy, the land, the community, the future? What images do they reach for?
- **Causality:** What causes what, in their account? Who has agency? Who is acted upon?
- **Absences:** What doesn't come up? What would feel strange or inappropriate to say? What is outside the frame?
- **Language:** What specific words do they use — and which words do they avoid? What terms carry weight?

Good narrative listening instruments create space for this deeper layer to emerge — they don't ask "what do you think about X?" but "tell me about a time when..." or "how would you describe X to someone who didn't know it?"

## Interview Guide Design

### Principles

**Open before specific.** Start with broad, open questions that let the person tell their own story before narrowing to the themes in your research parameters.

**Concrete before abstract.** Ask about specific experiences, events, or examples before asking about general views or beliefs. Stories reveal frames better than opinions do.

**Listen for language.** The exact words a person uses matter as much as what they say. Note recurring terms, metaphors, and the language they reach for when describing difficult or abstract things.

**Follow the unexpected.** The most valuable moments in a narrative interview are when something surprising appears — a word you didn't expect, a connection you didn't anticipate. Design space for that.

**End with reflection.** Close questions that invite the person to step back and reflect on the conversation — what they said, what matters, what they want to add.

### Interview Guide Template

```
## Interview Guide
**Project:** [project name]
**Small Listening Action:** [from Listening Model]
**Research Question:** [from Listening Model]
**Estimated duration:** [30–60 minutes recommended for narrative interviews]

---

### Opening (5 min)
Brief introduction: who you are, what this research is for, how findings will be used,
confidentiality, permission to take notes / record.

"Before we start, do you have any questions about the research or how your
responses will be used?"

---

### Warm-up — Their Story (10 min)
[Open questions that let the person situate themselves in relation to the topic.
Not about the narrative space — about them.]

1. "Can you tell me a little about your work / involvement with [topic area]?
   How did you come to be involved?"

2. "What drew you to this work / this community / this issue?"

[Adapt to context. The goal is to hear how they describe themselves in relation
to the topic — what role they give themselves, what language they use.]

---

### Core Questions — Narrative Listening (20–30 min)
[These are oriented toward the specific research question and parameters.
Generate 4–6 questions from the template below, adapted to the project.]

**Experience and story questions:**
- "Can you tell me about a specific moment when [topic] felt very present for you?
  What happened?"
- "What would you point to as a turning point in how [issue] has played out in
  [community / region / sector]?"

**Language and framing questions:**
- "How would you describe [topic] to someone who knew nothing about it?"
- "What words do people use when they talk about [topic] in your community?
  Are there words you find yourself avoiding or pushing back on?"

**Causality and agency questions:**
- "In your view, why is [situation] the way it is? What got us here?"
- "Who has the power to change this? What would need to happen?"

**What is missing / invisible questions:**
- "What do you think gets left out of how [topic] is usually talked about?"
- "Is there something important about [issue] that you rarely hear people say
  out loud — even if you think it's true?"

**Alternative narrative questions:**
- "Are there communities or places where [topic] is being talked about or
  approached differently? What does that look like?"
- "If things were going well — if [the change your POV points toward] were
  actually happening — what would that look like from where you sit?"

---

### Closing — Reflection (5–10 min)
- "Is there anything you expected me to ask that I didn't?"
- "Is there something you've been thinking about during our conversation
  that you'd like to add?"
- "Is there someone else you think I should speak with, or something you'd
  suggest I read or look at?"

---

### Post-Interview Notes (for researcher — fill in immediately after)
- What recurring language or phrases did this person use?
- What surprised you?
- What felt absent — what didn't come up that you expected?
- What frames or metaphors appeared?
- What does this add to or challenge from what you already know about this community?
```

## Observation Framework

Use when the Small Listening action involves attending an event, following a community space, or observing a public process — not conducting interviews.

### Principles

**Observe what is said and how, not just what.** The language used in a meeting, a protest, a community gathering is as important as the content of what is decided or demanded.

**Note what is absent.** What topics don't come up? Who doesn't speak? What would feel out of place here?

**Track recurring language.** What terms, phrases, or metaphors come up repeatedly? What words carry weight in this space?

**Note who speaks and who is listened to.** Power is visible in who gets the floor, who gets challenged, whose framing sticks.

```

**Observer:** [who is observing]

---

- Specific themes or language to listen for:

---

### During (structured notes)

**Who is present:**
[Who is in the space — communities, roles, demographics]

**What is said — language notes:**
[Recurring terms, phrases, metaphors. Quote directly where possible.
Note who said what.]

**What frames appear:**
[What is taken for granted? What causal logic appears?

**What is absent:**
[Topics that don't come up. People who don't speak.

[Who speaks? Who is listened to? Whose framing sticks?
```

## Survey Design for Narrative Research

Surveys are the weakest instrument for narrative listening — closed questions produce data about opinions, not frames. Use surveys only when scale matters and qualitative depth is not the priority. If possible, combine a short survey with follow-up interviews for a subset of respondents.

### Principles

**Open questions over scales.** Rating scales tell you intensity, not meaning. Open text fields, even short ones, reveal language and framing.

**Avoid leading language.** The words in your question prime the answer. Use neutral terms and test questions with someone from the community before distributing.

**Ask about experience before opinion.** "Have you experienced X?" before "What do you think about X?" grounds responses in concrete reality.

**Leave space for what isn't in your questions.** Always include a final open field: "Is there anything else you'd like to share?"

### Survey Template Structure

```
## Survey
**Project:** [project name]
**Estimated completion time:** [5–10 minutes recommended]

---

Section 1 — About You (optional, anonymous options available)
[2–3 questions about the respondent's relationship to the topic —
role, geography, how long involved. Do not ask for identifying information
unless necessary and consented to.]

Section 2 — Experience
[2–3 questions about concrete experience of the topic.
"Have you experienced X?" / "Can you describe a recent example of X?"]

Section 3 — Language and Framing
[2–3 open-text questions oriented toward narrative listening.
"How would you describe [topic] in your own words?"
"What words do you hear most often when people talk about [topic]?
Are there any you push back on?"]

Section 4 — What Is Missing
[1–2 questions about what gets left out.
"What do you think is missing from how [topic] is usually discussed?"]

Section 5 — Open
"Is there anything else you'd like to share about [topic] that we haven't asked about?"

---

### Post-Survey Analysis Notes
When processing survey responses into the CSV:
- Look for recurring language across open-text fields — these are frame signals
- Note outlier responses — unexpected framings are often the most useful
- Flag any responses that directly confirm or contradict Big Listening findings
- Assign Folio IDs to notable responses before entering them into the CSV
```

## Small Listening for Small Listening-Only Projects

When a project runs on Small Listening only — no Big Listening — the instrument design becomes even more important because it is the primary source of all research findings.

In this case, design at least two different instrument types — for example, interviews plus observation, or interviews plus a survey — to triangulate findings. Single-method Small Listening projects are more vulnerable to researcher bias and narrow sampling.

At the end of a Small Listening-only project, always ask: what would Big Listening add? The answer becomes part of the Handoff Checklist for Agent 03 and informs the next cycle's Listening Model update in Agent 02a.
