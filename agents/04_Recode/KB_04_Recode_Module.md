## What This Phase Is

Recode is the fourth phase of the Culture Hack Method. It is where analysis becomes strategy, and strategy becomes language.

The phase moves through five connected moments:

**Audience + Objectives** — Define who the hack needs to reach and what it aims to shift. These are co-defined: choosing an objective is choosing an audience. Both must be clear before decode/recode work can begin.

**Decode** — Identify a representative message — a paragraph-length text that carries the logic and framing being challenged. Systematically map its hidden structure: verbs, subjects, images, assumptions, logic, ideology, metaphors, framing. Each element reveals a political choice embedded in the language.

**Recode** — Build an alternative narrative using the same analytical lens. Every element is consciously rebuilt to open different imaginaries, guided by the audience and objectives.

**Message** — Translate the recoded narrative into exact language: the specific words, in a specific order, that activate the new frames for the target audience. The message is one to three paragraphs — it may read more like a manifesto than a slogan, and that is correct. This is the full structure of meaning the hack builds on. The meme, slogan, or condensed cultural unit comes later, in Agent 05a.

**Narrative Strategy** — The standalone synthesis. Integrates audience, objectives, and one or more decode/recode analyses into an action plan: short-term actions (what becomes the Hacking Tree) and long-term hypotheses (what could shift in the narrative space). Always checked against the POV — the strategy is a compass reading, not just a plan.

**Stand-alone use:** This phase can also be used independently of the full cycle — for example, to decode/recode a campaign message, test existing communications, or run a workshop exercise. In stand-alone mode, the Narrative Strategy is optional. Only Decode → Recode → Message is required.

## Key Concepts

**Audience vs. Narrative Community**
A Narrative Community (from Agent 03) is the analytical unit — a cluster of actors sharing frames and language, positioned on the Justice/Ontology map. The **Audience** is more specific: the particular people within or across communities that the hack needs to reach. A community is an analytical category; an audience is a person you can picture — with an age, a location, habits, feelings, and a relationship to the issue. The same community may contain multiple distinct audience segments. Hack design works at the people level, not the conceptual level.

**Audience Coherence**
A hack cannot speak simultaneously to audiences with incompatible logics, values, or cultural positions. If a practitioner identifies two audiences that would require contradictory messages, they must either choose one or design separate interventions — each with its own decode/recode thread and its own hack actions. The agent should name this as a strategic choice, not a problem to smooth over.

**Audience Objectives + Social Tipping Point**
For each audience, define: what shift is being sought, why this audience has the potential to move, and what their catalytic capacity is. Catalytic capacity is the potential for movement in this audience to ripple outward — into other communities, other conversations, broader narrative change. This connects to the **Social Tipping Point**: the theory that influencing 10–25% of a conversation can create a chain reaction that shifts the whole. Some audiences are strategically important precisely because they sit at this threshold — they are already connected to the right networks and can carry the reframe further than their own numbers suggest. The strategy question is not just "does this audience shift?" but "does their shift activate catalytic capacity that could cascade?" This is not mandatory for every hack, but it is always worth asking — and the answer shapes how the strategy is designed.

**Representative Message**
The specific text chosen for decode/recode. It must be paragraph-length — long enough to contain the logic, framing, and ideological structure being challenged. The message should already be visible in the 02b/03 outputs: a media excerpt, a campaign text, a community quote, a public statement. If the practitioner wants to decode their own existing message, that is a valid use of the same tool. If no clear message has been identified, the agent helps find or construct one before analysis begins.

**Decode**
Systematic analysis of a narrative's linguistic architecture through eight lenses: Verbs · Subjects & Objects · Evoked Images · Hidden Assumptions · Logic · Ideology · Metaphor · Framing. The goal is not to refute the narrative argument by argument — it is to reveal its hidden structure so the recode can work at the same deep level.

**Recode**
The construction of an alternative narrative using the same eight lenses. Every element is consciously rebuilt, guided by the audience and objectives. The recode is not a mirror inversion of the decode — it is a new construction built from different foundations, shaped by who it needs to reach and what shift it aims to produce.

**Narrative Objectives**
Specific goals for what the intervention aims to shift — framed around audiences, frames, and direction of movement. They include:
- Which audiences to engage and why
- What direction of movement is sought on the Justice/Ontology map
- Which frames to challenge, strengthen, or introduce
- Where the recoded narrative sits in the Window of Discourse and where it aims to move

**Narrative Strategy**
The standalone action plan that emerges from all the work above. It has two layers:
- **Short-term:** the specific intervention to develop in Agent 05a (the first hack)
- **Long-term:** iteration hypotheses — what could shift over time in language, attention, network, or power

The strategy should also surface the **implied effects** of the objectives: if this hack works, what would be visible? The practitioner may have a clear hypothesis, or may hold it open as an observation-based approach (like an A/B test). Both are valid. The agent helps formulate the effects rather than demanding certainty.

## The Eight Lenses (Decode + Recode)

| Element | Question |
|---------|----------|
| **Verbs — Actions** | What actions are present? Who acts, who is acted upon? What actions are made invisible? |
| **Subjects & Objects — Actors** | Who has agency? Who is absent? What power dynamics are encoded in who does what to whom? |
| **Evoked Images** | What mental pictures, sensory associations, and emotional resonances does this language produce? |
| **Hidden Assumptions** | What must already be believed for this message to make sense? What questions are not allowed? |
| **Logic — Truth Claims** | What is stated as fact? What causal logic is embedded? What counts as evidence? |
| **Ideology — Worldview** | What larger belief system does this serve? What future does it imply? What does it protect? |
| **Metaphor** | What is the primary metaphor? What does it make thinkable — and what does it foreclose? |
| **Framing — Point of View** | Whose perspective is centred? What is inside the frame and what is outside? Whose interests does this serve? After the analysis, distil the whole into one sentence that captures the coherent worldview — e.g. *"economic growth is more important than life"* or *"development is civilisation."* For the recode, produce the reverse synthesis — e.g. *"life is more important than money"* or *"the future is a territory to defend."* This distilled frame is the most actionable output of the analysis. |

## Documentation Questions

**On audience and objectives**
- Who exactly is the hack trying to reach? Describe them as people — not just a category.
- What is the relationship between the practitioner and this audience? What access or connection already exists?
- What shift are you seeking in this audience? What does movement look like for them?
- What is the catalytic potential of this audience — if they shift, what else could move?
- If you have named more than one audience: are these coherent? Do they require the same message, or different ones?

**On finding the representative message**
- Looking at the 02b/03 outputs: is there a quote, excerpt, or text that captures the dominant narrative being challenged?
- Does this text contain the logic, framing, and assumptions you want to work against?
- Is it paragraph-length — specific enough to analyse, not just a single phrase?
- If you are decoding your own existing message: what prompted this, and what do you suspect it might be doing unintentionally?

**On Decode**
- Walk through the eight lenses: what do you find in each?
- What does this narrative make invisible? What does it make seem natural or inevitable?
- What ideology does it serve, and what future does it foreclose?
- Which lens reveals the most important strategic insight?

**On Recode**
- For each element: what changes, why, and what effect is intended?
- What new frames and metaphors are being activated?
- Is the recode building from the audience's own language and cultural references?
- What is the exact message — the specific words that carry this reframe?

**On Narrative Strategy**
- What is the short-term action this strategy leads to? What gets handed to Agent 05a?
- If there is more than one decode/recode: how do they integrate into one strategy?
- What are the long-term hypotheses? What could shift in attention, network, power, or language if this works?
- Are these hypotheses specific, or held open for observation? Either is valid — name which.
- What does the practitioner need to see in Agent 05b's evaluation to know this strategy is working?

## What Comes Before / After

**Before:** Receives **Community Analysis Report + Narrative Map + Window of Discourse assessment** from Agent 03. The community map — including quadrant positions, Catalytic community identifications, and Window of Discourse placement — is the strategic foundation for setting audience, objectives, and building the reframe.

**After:** Passes **Narrative Strategy + Recoded Narrative (with Message)** — and optionally **Audience Profile(s)** — to **Agent 05a (Hacking Tree)**, which translates the message into a concrete cultural intervention.

**On iteration:** In a second or later cycle, the Recode is revised based on what the hack revealed. New community insights may shift target audiences, frame priorities, or the message itself. The POV may also be sharpened here. The strategy's long-term hypotheses are tested against what Agent 05b found and updated accordingly.
