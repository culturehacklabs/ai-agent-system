## What Output D Is

The Case Study is movement knowledge — a 4–6 page narrative written to be shared with others for learning and iteration. It tells the story of the hack: what was done, what shifted, what was learned, and what comes next.

It is written *with* the practitioner, not *for* them. The agent proposes language drawn from the documentation (Output C or equivalent). The practitioner approves, adjusts, and adds. Nothing is invented.

**Requires:** Output C (Hack Database) or equivalent documentation covering the full cycle. If Output C is incomplete, the agent will identify the gaps before producing the case study.

**Optional Rhizome Fellowship fields** are marked clearly — include only if working within the Fellowship context.

**Tone:** The case study is not an academic report. It is a practitioner narrative — clear, grounded, honest about what didn't work, and oriented toward what others can learn from it.

## Case Study Structure

### Page 1 — Who We Are + The Context

```
## [Hack Name] — Case Study

**Collective:** [who — name, brief description, where based]
**Period:** [dates — from POV development to evaluation]
**Pathway:** [optional — Rhizome Fellowship only]
**Issue:** [one sentence — what narrative space was being hacked]

---

### Our Point of View

[Extended POV — collective identity, narrative observation, vision, theory of change.
Written in the practitioner's own voice. The agent proposes from the Database; the practitioner adjusts.]

### The Narrative Space We Entered

[2–3 paragraphs describing the narrative landscape before the hack:
- What dominant narratives were in circulation
- What communities were involved and where they sat
- What the Window of Discourse looked like
- Why this space needed hacking — what the POV was responding to]
```

### Page 2 — What We Did: The Strategy

```
### Our Audience

[Who specifically the hack was designed to reach — described as people, not categories.
Their relationship to the issue, their cultural ground, their catalytic potential.]

### Our Objectives

[What shifts were being sought — in audience, in discourse, in the narrative space.
What hypotheses the strategy was testing.]

### The Frame We Challenged

[The decoded frame synthesis — one line.
Brief description of the representative message decoded and what it was doing.]

### The Frame We Built

[The recoded frame synthesis — one line.
The recoded message — full text, 1–3 paragraphs.]
```

### Page 3 — The Hack

```
### The Hacking Tree

**Media:** [format and why — one paragraph]

**Meme:** [the cultural unit — what it was, how the symbol was remixed, why it was sticky]

**Tone:** [the emotional strategy — what feelings were designed, what journey the audience was invited on]

**Hack:** [space, scale, and moment — where, how big, when, and why that combination]

### How It Came to Life

[1–2 paragraphs: the production and launch story. Who was involved. What happened.
What it looked like in the world. Any creative or logistical surprises.]
```

### Page 4 — What Shifted

```
### Level 1 — Audience Impact

[What the hack reached and how people responded.
Quantitative signals and qualitative stories.
Early adoption indicators if visible.
If A/B tested: what the comparison revealed.]

### Level 2 — Discourse Impact

[What shifted in attention, network, and power — compared against baseline.
Window of Discourse movement, even incremental.
New connections or bridges that emerged.]

### Level 3 — Systems Change Contribution

[Early signals of broader shift.
How this hack connects to other movements in the space.
What tipping point conditions it is contributing to.
Honest about timescale — not overclaiming.]
```

### Page 5 — What We Learned

```
### What Worked

[Be specific — which element of the Hacking Tree performed best and why.
What about the strategy proved effective.]

### What Didn't Work

[Be honest — what failed, underperformed, or produced unexpected negative responses.
What would be done differently.]

### What Surprised Us

[Anything unexpected — positive or negative — that the team didn't anticipate.]

### For Others Doing This Work

[2–4 practical lessons for other narrative practitioners working in a similar space.
Written as direct guidance, not abstraction.]
```

### Page 6 — Looking Forward

```
### The Next Cycle

[What the evaluation revealed about what the next hack needs.
Which phase of the method needs the most attention — and what will be done differently.]

### Three Reflections

*In the practitioner's own words.*

**For me personally:**
[Implications of this work for the practitioner — what was learned, what changed, what remains.]

**For my community:**
[Implications for the community this work serves — what shifted, what is now possible, what is still needed.]

**For the planet:**
[Implications for the larger ecological and social transformation this work is pointed toward — what this hack contributed to, what still needs to happen.]

### Rhizome Fellowship Reflection *(optional — Fellowship context only)*
[Connection to the Pathway. What this work contributed to the broader Fellowship ecosystem. What the practitioner is carrying forward into future work.]
```

## Writing Principles

**Voice** — The case study should sound like the practitioner, not like a report. The agent proposes language; the practitioner owns it.

**Honesty** — What didn't work is as valuable as what did. The case study is for learning, not for showcasing success. Honest failure is more useful to the movement than polished victory.

**Specificity** — Vague claims ("the hack reached many people") undermine credibility. Use real numbers, real quotes, real examples wherever possible.

**No invention** — The agent never invents facts, quotes, or outcomes. If something is unknown or undocumented, it says so and asks the practitioner to fill it in.

**Length** — 4–6 pages. Longer than a summary, shorter than a report. Each page should be substantive but readable in 2–3 minutes.
