## Power Prompts (when stuck)

**The practitioner is describing a material condition, not a narrative**

Use this to redirect:
> "What you've described is the condition you want to change. Now let's find the narrative underneath it. What story does the dominant culture tell about *why* things are this way — and *why* they should stay that way? What frame makes this feel natural or inevitable?"

**The collective identity is a generic label**

Use this to sharpen:
> "When you say 'we are activists / researchers / a nonprofit' — what specifically defines your relationship to this narrative space? Are you inside it, challenging it from outside, or working within communities most affected by it? The identity should tell us *why you're the ones doing this hack*."

**The theory of change is a programme theory, not a narrative theory**

Use this to redirect:
> "What you've described is what you hope to achieve programmatically. The theory of change in the POV answers a different question: *why does changing the narrative matter for that transformation?* What becomes possible — politically, culturally, socially — when the story shifts?"

**The Short POV is too broad to generate a research question**

Use this to tighten:
> "If I handed this Short POV to a researcher and said 'design a study of this narrative space' — could they? What specifically would they study? If it's not clear, the POV needs to name the *specific frame* being challenged and the *specific shift* being sought."

**The practitioner is mixing personal and collective voice**

Use this to clarify:
> "The POV is written in the first-person plural — 'we', not 'I'. It speaks for the collective undertaking the hack. Is there a group, movement, or organisation you're doing this with? Let's write the identity from that collective's position."

## Example POV (reference — do not reproduce verbatim)

**Context:** A collective of community land trust advocates in a mid-sized city.

**Extended POV:**
We are a coalition of community land trust advocates and tenant organisers who have spent years watching our city's working-class neighbourhoods hollowed out by speculative development. We see that the dominant narrative frames urban land as a private investment vehicle — an asset whose rising value is treated as a public good, even as it displaces the people who made those neighbourhoods liveable. This narrative makes gentrification feel like gravity: inevitable, natural, no one's fault. Therefore we want a public story in which land is understood as a shared inheritance — something held in trust for communities, not extracted from them. To do this, we must seed the frame of land-as-commons into the mainstream narrative of our city, making it legible, desirable, and politically achievable for the communities who need it most.

**Short POV:**
We are community land trust advocates challenging the dominant narrative that urban land is a private investment asset. We believe that seeding a narrative of land-as-commons — already alive in our communities — can shift the political conditions for tenure security and community ownership in our city.
