## Primary Output: Point of View Document

Use this template to produce the full POV document. Every field has inline guidance — remove the guidance text before delivering to the practitioner.

```
## Point of View

### Extended POV

[One paragraph following the four-part structure. Written in the first-person plural.
Should read as a coherent statement of collective purpose — not four answers stitched
together. Narrative observation must name a frame or cultural construct, not just a
material condition. Theory of change must explain why narrative is the lever, not just
what the collective hopes to achieve.]

---

**Collective Identity:**
[Who we are — the group, movement, or collective. Grounded in their relationship to
the narrative space. Not a generic label. Example: "We are a coalition of grassroots
housing organisers working in the communities most affected by speculative development."]

**Narrative Observation:**
[What we see in the narrative landscape — the dominant frame or cultural construct being
challenged. What it makes visible. What it hides. How it naturalises the conditions we
want to change. Example: "The dominant narrative frames housing as a financial asset
whose value must be protected — making displacement feel like a natural consequence of
market forces rather than a political choice."]

**Vision:**
[What we want to shift — what becomes possible when the narrative changes. Not a policy
goal. A statement of different sense-making. Example: "A public narrative in which
housing is understood as a commons, a right, and a site of collective life — where
security of tenure is the baseline expectation, not a privilege."]

**Theory of Change:**
[How changing this narrative contributes to the larger transformation. The bridge between
POV and hack. Why narrative is the lever here. Example: "When housing is understood as
a commons rather than a commodity, the political conditions for systemic tenure reform
become thinkable — and the grassroots movements demanding it become legible as common
sense rather than radical fringe."]

---

### Short POV

[1–3 sentences. The direct handoff to Agent 02a. Must be specific enough to orient a
research question. Should name: who we are, what narrative we are challenging, and what
shift we are working toward. A reader should know exactly what narrative space is being
hacked and why.]

---

### Pathway *(Rhizome Fellowship only — delete if not applicable)*

[One of the Five Pathways this POV connects to, with one sentence on why:]
- Restoring Justice, Restoring Life
- Economies of Regeneration
- Land Back to Right Relationship
- Bioregional Community Governance
- Healing the Spirit, Healing the Earth

---

### Timeline *(optional — imagination exercise, not a plan)*

[A few sentences on where this narrative shift could lead over the next few years and
beyond. Written as imaginative possibility, not a fixed roadmap. Helps the collective
see the longer arc of their work.]
```

## Poetic Manifesto

*Activated when the user requests a "poetic manifesto" or arrives with transcript/assembly notes.*

---

### What It Is

A poetic manifesto is a collective text woven from the actual words of participants in a group process — an assembly, a workshop, a gathering. It is not a summary, a synthesis, or an interpretation. It is a poem-like arrangement of what people said, preserving their language, their rhythms, and their contradictions.

The result holds diversity over unity: it does not smooth differences into a single voice, but lets multiple voices coexist, call to each other, and sometimes pull in different directions. The political stance is strong, but it emerges from the participants' own words — not from the facilitator's framing.

---

### Rules

**Transcribe, do not interpret.**
Use the participants' actual words and phrases. Do not paraphrase, restate in cleaner language, or "improve" what they said. If something is ambiguous or raw, keep it that way. The roughness is often where the life is.

**Hear with the ears of a poet.**
Listen for: the image that lands. The phrase that carries weight. The word someone chose that no one else would have chosen. The moment something unexpected was said. The silence around a word. These are the things to keep.

**Weave, do not stack.**
The manifesto is not a list of quotes. It is a text where voices move in and out of each other — sometimes finishing each other's thoughts, sometimes in tension. Line breaks, white space, and repetition are tools. The arrangement is itself an act of authorship.

**Diversity over unity.**
Resist the urge to resolve tensions between what different people said. If one person said "we need to fight" and another said "we need to heal", both stay. The contradiction is the truth of the collective.

**Political stance through language.**
The manifesto should have a clear political direction — but that direction must come from the participants' own words, not from frames introduced by the facilitator or agent. If participants didn't use the frame, it doesn't appear.

**Length.**
Aim for 2–5 pages. Maximum 8 pages if the material is rich and the group was large. If the transcript is longer, the work is curation — selecting the passages that carry the most weight and weaving them most tightly.

---

### Process

**Step 1 — Receive the material**
Ask if not already provided:
- How many people participated?
- What was the context (assembly, workshop, kitchen table, etc.)?
- Were the questions asked to the group, or did it emerge from open conversation?
- Is this a full transcript or notes taken by a listener?

**Step 2 — Read with attention**
Before selecting anything, read the full material through once. Note: recurring words or images, phrases that surprise, moments of intensity, things that were said more than once (by different people), tensions that were not resolved.

**Step 3 — Select**
Choose the passages that carry the most weight. Prioritise: specificity over generality, image over abstraction, the particular word over the common word, emotional truth over logical clarity.

**Step 4 — Arrange**
Build the text. Use line breaks intentionally. Let the movement of the text mirror the movement of collective sense-making: from observation to feeling to demand to vision, or whatever arc the material itself suggests. Do not impose an arc that wasn't there.

**Step 5 — Review**
Before delivering: Is every line traceable to something a participant said? Are there any words or frames introduced by the agent that weren't in the material? If yes — remove them.

### Translation: Poetic Manifesto → Technical POV

When the practitioner asks to translate the manifesto into a Technical POV:

1. Re-read the manifesto and identify which passages carry each of the four POV elements:
   - **Identity** — how participants described themselves or their collective
   - **Narrative Observation** — what they said about the dominant story, the frame, the cultural construct
   - **Vision** — what they said about what they wanted, what they imagined
   - **Theory of Change** — how they described their role, their method, their belief in why narrative matters

2. Draft the Extended POV using the participants' own language as much as possible. The technical structure is the container; their words are the content.

3. Draft the Short POV as a 1–3 sentence distillation — still drawing from the manifesto's language where possible.

4. Note any gaps: if the manifesto material doesn't clearly answer one of the four elements, flag it and ask the practitioner to fill it from their own knowledge of the group.

**What the agent does not do in translation:**
Introduce frames, vocabulary, or political positions that were not present in the manifesto. The translation moves from poetic to technical form — it does not add substance.
