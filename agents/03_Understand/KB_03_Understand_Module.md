## What This Phase Is

Understand is the third phase of the Culture Hack Method. It transforms the research package from Agent 02b into strategic intelligence about the narrative space.

The phase applies four analytical tools in sequence:

1. **ANP Analysis** — Attention, Network, Power: evidence-based assessment of each community's presence and position in the narrative space, grounded in the ANP Source Library from Agent 02b
2. **Language Analysis** — Frames, metaphors, and ideological logic: the cognitive architecture that organises how each community makes sense of the issue
3. **Community Mapping** — Positioning communities on the Justice/Ontology axes, reviewed and confirmed by the practitioner through the Map Check-in
4. **Window of Discourse** — Identifying which narratives are mainstream, emerging, or marginalised, and where intervention can shift the window

Together these produce the **Community Analysis Report** and the **Narrative Map** — the strategic foundation that Agent 04 builds its strategy on.

## Key Concepts

**Narrative Community**
A group of actors persistently engaged in conversation about a specific topic over time, using shared language, frames, and belief systems. Three types:
- **Hegemonic** — reinforces dominant narratives and existing power structures
- **Alternative** — proposes different stories; exists at the fringes of mainstream
- **Catalytic** — has high potential to drive broader narrative shifts and bridge communities

Note on Cooptation: communities that perform progressive or pluralistic language while their actual practices reinforce systems of oppression are typed as `alternative` in the mapper tool but positioned in the **Injustice + Ontofake** quadrant. They are not a fourth community type — they are a specific and dangerous configuration that requires practitioner knowledge to identify correctly. Automated analysis will almost always misread them as Catalytic or Justice + Onto-shift. See the critical Ontofake note in the Justice/Ontology Map section below.

**ANP Analysis**
Three criteria for assessing narrative communities. Each has a deep-dive structure:

*Attention — volume, reach, and resonance:*
- Content volume: frequency of narrative production over the research timeframe
- Reach: how far narratives travel beyond the immediate community
- Resonance: which themes generate the most activity and sharing
- Temporal patterns: is the community's narrative growing, declining, or stable?
- Comparative: how does this community's attention level compare to others in the space?

*Network — social structure:*
- Key nodes: which actors or organisations are most central
- Density: how tightly connected is the community internally
- Topology: centralised (hub-and-spoke), decentralised (multiple centres), or distributed (flat)
- Bridge actors: who moves between this community and others — these are strategically important
- Information flow: how do ideas spread within and beyond this community

*Power — influence and centrality:*
- Institutional power: media access, economic resources, policy influence
- Cultural power: legitimacy, credibility, symbolic authority — whose framing is treated as common sense
- Network power: coalition capacity, mobilisation ability, ability to bring others into alignment
- Overall power level: High / Medium / Low — with reasoning

Communities with the most attention and network interactions over time have the most power. Power is relative — assess it in comparison to other communities in this space.

**Folio IDs in ANP Analysis**
The ANP Assessment is the evidence-based layer of the analysis. Every claim must cite the Folio IDs from the ANP Source Library that support it. Example: "This community has high institutional power, with consistent placement in mainstream media outlets [BL-H-003, BL-H-007]."

**Language Analysis — Frames and Metaphors**
Systematic analysis of the cognitive architecture of each community's narrative. This is interpretive work — it draws on the research data and practitioner knowledge but involves analytical judgment that goes beyond what can be sourced. Folio IDs are not required in this section.

Three frame questions:
1. *What is inside the frame?* — what is included, made visible, centred
2. *What is outside the frame?* — what is excluded, made invisible, dismissed
3. *How is what's inside constructed?* — how common sense is created; what makes certain ideas feel natural, inevitable, or acceptable, and others unthinkable

Alongside frames, identify:
- **Dominant metaphors** — conceptual mappings that ground abstract ideas in familiar experiences. What does the metaphor make thinkable? What does it foreclose? (e.g. "the economy is a machine" enables efficiency-thinking but disables care-thinking)
- **Ideological logic** — the worldview tying frames together into a coherent system with internal justification

What is absent in the discourse often says more than what is present.

**Justice/Ontology Map**
A two-axis map for positioning narrative communities:
- **Justice axis (X, 0–100):** 0 = Injustice/Individual/Reform end · 100 = Justice/Systemic/Transformative end
- **Ontology axis (Y, 0–100):** 0 = Monoculture/Singular ways of knowing · 100 = Onto-shift/Plural ways of knowing. High = more plural, top of map. Low = more singular, bottom of map.

**Note for the mapper tool:** The current mapper tool inverts the ontology axis (high value = bottom). A tool update is required to match this intuitive convention. Until updated, use the conversion in `KB_03_Understand_Tools.md` when generating JSON coordinates.

Four quadrants:
- **Upper Right (Justice + Onto-shift):** justice > 50, ontology > 50 — systems change + plural ways of knowing. Target direction for culture change.
- **Upper Left (Injustice + Ontofake):** justice < 50, ontology > 50 — structural oppression performing plural/change language. See critical note below.
- **Lower Right (Justice + Reform):** justice > 50, ontology < 50 — structural change through conventional knowledge
- **Lower Left (Injustice + Monoculture):** justice < 50, ontology < 50 — oppression + singular ways of knowing

The axes can be adapted to fit the POV's specific goals — the framework is a tool, not a fixed grid.

**Critical note — Injustice + Ontofake quadrant:**
This is the trickiest quadrant in the map. Ontofake communities use the language of ontological shift — plurality, relationality, Indigenous epistemology, care — while their actual practices and structures keep systems of oppression intact. Cooptation is the mechanism: progressive discourse absorbs and neutralises challenge rather than enabling it.

Automated analysis is structurally biased toward misreading Ontofake communities as Catalytic or Justice + Onto-shift — because the language looks right and the metaphors can look right. Only practitioner knowledge of actual practices, structural effects, and community context reveals the cooptation. This is why the Map Check-in is a co-creation moment, not an automated output.

When analysing a suspected Ontofake community in Language Analysis, apply a specific additional lens: **the gap between discourse and practice.** Ask: What does this community say versus what does it structurally do? Who benefits from the narrative it produces? Does the language of change function to absorb and neutralise dissent rather than enable transformation? Does progressive framing serve to maintain existing power arrangements?

**Acupuncture Points**
Specific communities, actors, moments, or connections in the narrative space where a targeted culture hack could have disproportionate impact — small pressure, significant shift. Named by analogy to acupuncture: the right intervention at the right point in the system produces effects beyond what the scale of the intervention would suggest. Acupuncture Points emerge from the intersection of the ANP analysis (where is there network bridging capacity?), the Language Analysis (where are the frame contradictions or openings?), and the Window of Discourse (what is close to tipping?). They are the most specific and actionable outputs of the Strategic Insights section.

**Window of Discourse**
The Window of Discourse is the range of narratives treated as acceptable in public debate at a given moment. Positioning the narrative space against it shows what is currently common sense, what is gaining ground, and what remains marginal. Three positions:
- **Mainstream/Acceptable** — dominant, treated as common sense
- **Emerging/Contested** — gaining traction, not yet mainstream
- **Radical/Unacceptable** — marginalised, treated as extreme or unthinkable

The window is represented on the Narrative Map as a draggable rectangle with coordinates:
- `left`: X position of the window's left edge (0–100, same as justice axis)
- `top`: Y position of the window's top edge (0–100, same ontology axis — 100 = Onto-shift, top of map)
- `width` and `height`: size of the window as percentage of map

The window is always moving. Social movements, cultural moments, and political crises shift what sits inside or outside it. Identifying where the practitioner's desired reframe currently sits — and what would move the window — is the strategic conclusion of this phase.

## The Analytical Sequence

### Step 1 — Confirm Community First Drafts

From the Narrative Report, confirm the community first drafts from Agent 02b. For each community, review:
- Name and description
- Initial type (Hegemonic / Alternative / Catalytic)
- Whether the practitioner agrees with the initial identification

Adjust names, descriptions, or types as needed. If the practitioner identifies that a community is more complex than initially mapped — for example, two distinct sub-communities were grouped together — split it here before proceeding. 3–5 communities is the working range.

### Step 2 — ANP Analysis

For each community, work through the full deep-dive structure. Use the ANP Source Library entries as evidence — cite Folio IDs. Produce an ANP Assessment for each community covering all three dimensions with sub-lenses. Compare communities relatively: which has most attention, most power, most bridging capacity?

Bridge actors are particularly important — identify any actors who appear in multiple communities or move between them. These are strategic nodes for the Recode phase.

### Step 3 — Map Check-in

After ANP, before Language Analysis. This is the single co-creation moment in Agent 03. Load `KB_03_Understand_MapCheck.md`. Present the draft positioning, invite the practitioner to review and adjust. Proceed only after confirmation.

The Map Check-in is the pivot between evidence-based work (ANP) and interpretive work (Language Analysis). The positioning confirmed here feeds directly into the final Narrative Map JSON.

### Step 4 — Language Analysis

For each community, apply the three frame questions. Identify dominant metaphors and what they make thinkable or foreclose. Identify the ideological logic. Note what is absent — what the community doesn't say, what would feel out of place, what is treated as unthinkable.

For any community positioned in the **Injustice + Ontofake** quadrant, apply an additional lens: the gap between discourse and practice. Ask: What does this community say versus what does it structurally do? Who benefits from the narrative it produces? Does the language of change function to absorb and neutralise dissent rather than enable transformation?

*Note at the start of this section in the output: "The following analysis is interpretive — it draws on research data and practitioner knowledge but involves analytical judgment. The practitioner should review this section before passing to Agent 04."*

### Step 5 — Window of Discourse

Assess the current narrative space as a whole. Where does the dominant narrative sit? Where does the practitioner's desired reframe currently sit — Mainstream, Emerging, or Radical? What would move the window? Identify the Catalytic communities already pushing in the desired direction.

Determine the Window of Discourse rectangle coordinates for the Narrative Map — where does mainstream discourse currently sit on the Justice/Ontology axes?

### Step 6 — Strategic Insights + Narrative Intentions

Surface what the analysis points toward. Two layers: Strategic Insights (observations about the narrative space — what is possible, where frame openings exist, where Acupuncture Points are) and Narrative Intentions (options for Agent 04 — possible communities, frames, directions). See the Strategic Insights + Narrative Intentions section below for full guidance.

## Strategic Insights + Narrative Intentions

At the end of the Community Analysis Report, surface what the analysis points toward. This section has two layers:

**Strategic Insights** — what the analysis observes about the narrative space: where movement is possible, where frame openings exist, where the window is close to shifting, where Acupuncture Points are located. These are analytical observations, not decisions.

**Narrative Intentions** — possible directions that emerge from the insights: options for which communities to engage, which frames to challenge or amplify, what kind of movement to pursue. These are options, not commitments. Agent 04 turns Narrative Intentions into Narrative Objectives — the committed strategic plan.

The distinction matters: Strategic Insights describe the landscape. Narrative Intentions describe possible moves within it. Narrative Objectives are the decisions made. Agent 03 produces the first two. Agent 04 produces the third.

## Iteration Workflow

In a second or later cycle, existing Community Profiles are updated — not rebuilt from scratch.

**What stays stable:**
- Community names (unless the hack revealed a naming problem)
- The basic analytical structure of each profile

**What gets updated:**
- ANP data: new research cycle, new Folio IDs, updated reach/power/network assessment
- Language patterns: new frames or metaphors that emerged during or after the hack
- Justice/Ontology positioning: has any community shifted? Document movement as evidence of change
- Window of Discourse: has the window moved? What is now Emerging that was Radical?
- Community type: has any community shifted from Alternative to Catalytic, or gained/lost influence?

**`[Updated]` markers:**
Any field that changed from the previous cycle gets an `[Updated]` tag with a brief note on what changed and why. Example: `**Attention:** High [Updated — reach increased significantly after the hack period, new actors entering the conversation — BL-C2-H-004]`

**Split communities:**
If the hack revealed that a community is more complex than initially mapped — for example, two distinct sub-communities with different frames and positions — split it into more specific communities that better reflect what was learned. Document the split with a note explaining why.
