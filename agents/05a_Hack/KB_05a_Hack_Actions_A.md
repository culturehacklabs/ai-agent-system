## Branch 1 — Media: What form carries the message?

### 1.1 Audiences

**If Agent 04 outputs are available:**
Reference the Audience Profile(s) from Agent 04 directly. Ask: are these still the right audiences for this hack, or has anything shifted? If multiple audience profiles exist, confirm which one(s) this hack is designed to reach — only proceed with audiences that are close enough in cultural ground to be served by one intervention.

**If using Stand-alone Mode:**
Collect the light Audience Profile from the Module before proceeding. Essential fields only. Do not accept "the general public."

### 1.2 Intersections

Once audiences are confirmed, identify the shared cultural ground:
- What platforms, spaces, or media do they both use?
- What aesthetic, genre, or cultural references do they share?
- What experiences, values, or emotional states connect them?

This is the strategic input to format choice. The intersection is where the hack can land for both audiences simultaneously — without diluting the message for either.

## Branch 2 — Meme: What is the sticky cultural unit?

**Transition note:** Your message from Agent 04 is the full structure of meaning — 1 to 3 paragraphs carrying the recoded logic, framing, and worldview. The meme is what that message becomes in the hands of the audience: a hashtag, a slogan, an image, a gesture. It is the vessel. The whole narrative travels inside it without being stated. That is the memetic power — the logic jumps from mind to mind without the explanation.

### 2.1 Symbols

Identify existing cultural objects already in circulation in the audience's world:
- What symbols (flags, gestures, colours, words, images, figures) carry emotional or cultural weight?
- Which are already associated with the issue, the movement, or the community?
- Which are surprising — unexpected carriers that could reframe how the issue is perceived?

### 2.2 Associations

Remix the symbol's meaning:
- What does this symbol currently mean to the audience?
- What do you want it to mean — what new association activates the recoded frames?
- What is the bridge: how do you move from the old meaning to the new one?

### 2.3 Synthesis

Produce the exact cultural unit:

```
## Branch 2 — Meme

**Symbol:**
- Chosen symbol: [what it is]
- Current association: [what it means now to the audience]
- New association: [what you want it to mean]
- Bridge: [how the remix works — what connects old to new]
- Why this symbol: [why it carries weight for this audience]

**Synthesis:**
- The meme: [exact form — hashtag / slogan / image description / gesture / phrase]
- Form: [hashtag / slogan / visual / gesture / other]
- In one line: [state it]

**Stickiness test:**
- Simple: [can anyone grasp it instantly?]
- Unexpected: [does it surprise or intrigue?]
- Concrete: [is it specific and tangible?]
- Credible: [is it believable for this audience?]
- Emotional: [does it make people feel something?]
- Story: [does it carry a story or imply one?]

**Overall assessment:** [what's strong / what to sharpen]
```

## Branch 3 — Tone: What emotions carry the message?

### 3.1 Mood

Read the current emotional climate:
- What is the dominant feeling in the narrative space right now?
- What is the target audience feeling about this issue — fear, anger, despair, apathy, hope, determination?
- What emotional patterns are the competing narratives using?

### 3.2 Feelings to Generate

Design the emotional journey:
- What feelings does the hack aim to produce in the audience?
- What is the journey: from where they are now → through what transitional feeling → to where you want them?
- Why these feelings — how do they serve the strategy and lead to agency?

Tone must be empowering, not manipulative. The test: does the emotion produced lead to action, or does it leave people stuck in feeling?

## Branch 4 — Hack: Where, how big, and when?

### 4.1 Space

Choose where to intervene. Space choice carries symbolic meaning — it is not just a delivery mechanism, it is part of the message.

**Points of intervention (Beautiful Trouble framework):**
- **Point of production** — where things are made (factory, farm, studio, office)
- **Point of destruction** — where harm happens (landfill, clearcut, demolition site)
- **Point of consumption** — where things are bought or used (store, platform, public square)
- **Point of decision** — where power is exercised (government building, boardroom, institution)
- **Point of assumption** — where common sense is produced (media, education, culture)
- **Point of potential** — underutilised space where new things can grow

### 4.2 Scale

Be honest about reach. Ambition is good; overcommitment kills hacks.
- **Micro** — under 100 people (intimate, relational, high trust)
- **Meso** — 100–10,000 (community, network, organisation)
- **Macro** — 10k–1M (city, region, large platform)
- **Mega** — over 1M (national, international, mass scale)

Also assess: primary audience (core reach) + secondary audience (who else gets touched) + viral/replication potential.

### 4.3 Moment

Time the hack to maximise impact:
- **Planned timing** — a set date or period chosen in advance
- **Event-responsive timing** — a political crisis, movement surge, policy announcement, or cultural moment that creates an opening
- **Cyclical timing** — an anniversary, commemoration, ecological cycle, or political rhythm

The wave question: is there momentum already building that this hack can ride? When is it cresting? What is the backup if the planned moment shifts?
