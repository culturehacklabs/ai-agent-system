## What This Phase Is

Hack is the fifth phase of the Culture Hack Method. It is where the recoded narrative becomes a concrete cultural intervention — something that actually exists in the world and reaches an audience.

The phase works through two layers:

**The Hacking Tree** — four creative branches that translate the message into a cultural unit and design its delivery: Media (what format), Meme (what cultural unit), Tone (what emotions), and Hack (what space, scale, and moment).

**Logistics** — the practical plan that makes the hack real: timeline, team, capacities, budget, documentation, and monitoring.

**On creativity:** This phase is a co-creative moment. The agent helps brainstorm, structure, and sharpen — but the best hacks carry a human creative touch that no tool can produce alone. The practitioner should feel free to bring in artists, designers, or community members at any point in this phase. The Hacking Tree can serve as a creative brief handed to collaborators, or as a script for the practitioner's own creative process.

**On prototyping:** Culture hacking is iterative by nature. The goal is not a perfect intervention — it is a good-enough one that can be launched, learned from, and improved. A hack that ships and fails teaches more than one that never launches. Build in enough to start; the next cycle will sharpen it.

**Stand-alone use:** This phase can be used independently of the full cycle — for example, to design a campaign action, a workshop, or a one-off intervention. In stand-alone mode, the agent collects a light Audience Profile before proceeding. "The general public" is not a valid audience — the hack must be designed for specific people.

## Key Concepts

**The Hacking Tree**
The structured creative design of the intervention. Four branches, each with three sub-elements, each building on the previous. Together they answer: how does the message become an action in the world?

**Media**
The format and platform that carries the message to the audience. The best media is the one the audience already uses, understands, and shares — TikTok, hip hop, slam poetry, murals, food rituals, theatre, op-eds, billboards. Media choice is driven by audience habits, cultural ground, and the team's production capacity.

The Media branch has three sub-elements:
- **Audiences** — who specifically the hack reaches (one or more from Agent 04, or built in stand-alone mode)
- **Intersections** — the shared cultural ground between audience segments: the platforms they both use, the references they share, the aesthetic they both respond to. This is what makes a format choice strategic rather than arbitrary.
- **Format** — the specific medium chosen, with reasoning: why this format fits this audience, and whether the team can produce it

**Meme**
The message distilled into a simple cultural unit that can jump from mind to mind — something people can instantly grasp, share, and make their own. The meme is the vessel; the message from Agent 04 is the cargo. A hashtag, slogan, image, or gesture carries the full logic of the recoded narrative without stating it. That is the memetic power: the whole worldview travels inside a single shareable unit.

The Meme branch has three sub-elements:
- **Symbols** — existing cultural objects already in circulation that carry emotional or cultural weight for the audience
- **Associations** — remixing what those symbols mean: from their current association to the new one that activates the recoded frames
- **Synthesis** — the exact cultural unit: the hashtag, slogan, image, gesture, or phrase that carries the message in condensed, spreadable form

**Stickiness test (SUCCESs):** Simple · Unexpected · Concrete · Credible · Emotional · Story

**Tone**
The emotions evoked through the message and spread in the conversation. Just as films use sound, colour, and rhythm to shape feeling, the hack intentionally evokes specific sentiments. Tone is not decoration — it is strategy. Emotion leads to agency, not just feeling.

The Tone branch has three sub-elements:
- **Mood** — the current emotional climate of the narrative space and the target audience
- **Feelings** — the emotions the hack aims to generate, and the emotional journey from where the audience is now to where the hack wants to take them
- **Stimuli** — the specific creative tools (visual, sonic, linguistic, physical) that produce those feelings

Tone must be empowering and authentic. The test: does the emotion lead to action and agency, or does it trap people in feeling?

**Hack: Space, Scale, Moment**
The three dimensions of where, how big, and when:

- **Space** — where to intervene: physical public space, community space, virtual platform, institutional space, or hybrid. Space choice carries symbolic meaning. The Point of Intervention framework helps identify leverage: production, destruction, consumption, decision, assumption, or potential.
- **Scale** — the realistic reach: Micro (under 100), Meso (100–10,000), Macro (10k–1M), Mega (1M+). Scale must match the narrative ambition and the team's actual capacity.
- **Moment** — the political timing that maximises impact: planned timing, event-responsive timing (catching a crisis, movement surge, or cultural moment), or cyclical timing (commemorations, ecological cycles, political rhythms). The key question: is there a wave to catch?

**Logistics**
The practical infrastructure that lets the hack actually happen. Two tracks are available:
- **Lean logistics** — for teams that need speed: timeline essentials, roles, minimum budget, and a documentation baseline
- **Full logistics** — for teams that need depth: detailed timeline with phases, full team roles, capacity assessment, budget breakdown (minimum viable through optimal), documentation plan, and monitoring with response protocols

Logistics is a reality check: can the team actually do this? Ambition is good; overcommitment kills hacks. The logistics plan should be honest.

## Stand-alone Audience Profile (light)

When arriving without Agent 04 outputs, collect the following before starting the Hacking Tree:

**Essential (required):**
- Age, gender, location, political stance
- Narrative objective: what shift is being sought with this audience?

**Optional (deepen when relevant):**
- What do they hear about this issue?
- What do they see in daily life?
- What do they say about the topic?
- What do they think/feel (logics, values, feelings)?
- What do they do (actions related to the issue)?

"The general public" is not a valid audience. If the practitioner cannot describe their audience as specific people, the agent should help them narrow before proceeding.

## Documentation Questions

**On audience and media**
- Who exactly is the hack reaching? Are these the same audiences from Agent 04, or have they shifted?
- What do both/all audience segments have in common — platforms, aesthetics, cultural references, shared experiences?
- What format fits this audience and is within the team's real production capacity?

**On meme**
- What symbols are already active in the audience's world? Which carry emotional weight?
- What does the chosen symbol currently mean, and what do you want it to mean instead?
- What is the exact cultural unit — the hashtag, slogan, image, or gesture? Say it in one line.
- Does it pass the stickiness test: is it simple, unexpected, concrete, credible, emotional, story-like?

**On tone**
- What is the dominant emotional mood of the narrative space right now? What is your audience feeling?
- What feelings do you want to generate — and what is the journey from where they are to there?
- What specific creative tools (colours, rhythm, imagery, word register, music, physical movement) produce those feelings?
- Is the emotion empowering? Does it lead to agency?

**On space, scale, moment**
- Where are you intervening — and why does that space create leverage?
- What point of intervention is this: production, destruction, consumption, decision, assumption, or potential?
- What is the realistic scale — primary and secondary reach?
- Is there a wave to catch — a political crisis, movement surge, anniversary, or cultural moment? If so, when is it cresting?

**On logistics**
- What is the honest timeline from development through launch to evaluation?
- Who does what? Are there gaps in the team?
- What can the team do in-house? What needs to be sourced externally?
- What is the minimum viable budget — and what is secured?
- What needs to be documented before, during, and after — and how?

## What Comes Before / After

**Before:** Receives **Narrative Strategy + Recoded Narrative (with Message) + Audience Profile(s)** from Agent 04. The message, target audience, and strategy objectives are all direct inputs to the Hacking Tree design.

**After:** Passes the **Hacking Tree + Logistics Plan** to Agent 05b (Impact & Iteration). The full documentation package — baseline from Agents 02b and 03, narrative strategy from Agent 04, and the monitoring plan from this phase — travels with it.

**On iteration:** Hacks are iterative. Some things work and some don't. The logistics plan includes a documentation and monitoring layer precisely so that what is learned feeds the next cycle. After evaluation, the practitioner may return to Agent 02a to update the Listening Model, or to Agent 04 to revise the narrative strategy.
