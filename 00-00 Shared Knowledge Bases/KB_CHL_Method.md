## The Metacrisis and the Role of Culture

We are in the midst of a metacrisis. Inequality, ecological collapse, climate change, biodiversity loss, rising authoritarianism, and institutional racism are not just symptoms of systemic breakdown — they are the result of a deeper cultural paradigm.

Culture is the values, belief systems, and ontologies that drive human behaviour. It shapes how we relate to ourselves, each other, and the network of life. The dominant growth-driven, capitalist culture — particularly in its neoliberal phase — commodifies life and resources, reinforcing separation, hierarchy, white supremacy, and exploitation.

To address the metacrisis, we must transform the cultural narratives that sustain it.

**The deep structure of culture is the relationship between self and other.**
- **Self:** the unit of the conscious human
- **Other:** everything that is not self — other people, ecosystems, economies, land, the network of life

Cultures are full of constructs that define this relationship — worldviews, ontologies, frames, and metaphors. Dominant Western cultural constructs often frame anything non-human as separate from us, as resource or object. The narrative *"nature is a resource"* is one expression of this: it makes extraction feel natural and regeneration feel secondary. **Cultural constructs are carried through narratives. Narrative practitioners work to shift these constructs.**

## What Is a Culture Hack?

A culture hack is a narrative action designed to shift common sense toward justice and ontological shift. Culture Hack Labs (CHL) developed this methodology for practitioners working on the frontlines of systems change — to dismantle the narratives that justify oppression and seed the narratives that make regenerative futures thinkable.

**Narrative-led systems change:** When we shift narratives — our shared cognitive maps, beliefs, and values — we redefine what is possible, ultimately reshaping the systems those narratives sustain. This is not a communications strategy. It is an intervention in the systems of meaning that shape material reality.

## What Is a Narrative?

A narrative functions as a **map**. The map is not the territory — but the map determines what we see in the territory, what we consider worth naming, and what we consider possible within it.

Narratives simplify, highlight, and frame certain aspects of reality while obscuring others. Through this, they provide the justification and basis for our actions — much of it on an unconscious level.

### The Narrative Form Model

Narratives operate through a cycle connecting the visible and the invisible:

```
Actions & Expressions  →  Sensation  →  Concepts  →  Intentions  →  Actions & Expressions
(memes, speeches,          (perceived      (organized through                (next cycle)
posts, images, songs)       through the     frames + metaphors)
                            body)
```

- **Actions & Expressions** — the visible surface: a meme, a speech, a poster, a song, a post. What we can observe and collect.
- **Sensation** — how expressions are perceived and felt. Narratives enter through the body, not just the mind.
- **Concepts** — how sensation is organised into meaning, through frames and metaphors.
- **Intentions** — the orientations and motivations those concepts produce.
- **Actions & Expressions** — the output, which loops back into the next cycle.

This is why culture hacks work at the level of frames and metaphors, not just messages: changing what is said changes what is felt and thought, which changes what is done.

### The Components of a Narrative

**Frames** are largely subconscious mental schemas that allow us to immediately make sense of the world. They unify ideas, facts, emotions, memories, and assumptions into a coherent structure. To analyse a frame, ask: *1) What is inside the frame? 2) What is outside it? 3) How is what's inside constructed?* The third question asks how common sense is created — what makes certain ideas feel natural, inevitable, or acceptable, and others unthinkable. What is absent in discourse often says more than what is present.

**Metaphors** are the cognitive devices that form the basis of frames — understanding one thing in terms of another in ways that highlight some aspects and hide others. They are not decorative; they determine what is thinkable. ("The economy is a machine" enables efficiency-thinking but disables care-thinking.)

**Ideologies** bring multiple frames into larger structured relationships, coordinated through a system of internal logic that makes certain conclusions feel inevitable.

## Political Stance: Justice + Onto-shift

The Culture Hack Method is not neutral. It is designed explicitly for **Justice + Onto-shift**.

**Justice (systemic/transformative):** To move beyond the metacrisis, we must address structural inequities — challenging systems of oppression, redistributing power, and repairing harm. The method supports communities doing this work. It does not support narratives that maintain the status quo, even when they use progressive language. The goal is transformation, not reform within unjust systems.

**Onto-shift (ontological shift):** Justice alone is not enough. As philosopher Bayo Akomolafe has noted: *"Demands for social justice may get us a seat at the table, but they will never let us leave the house of modernity."* To truly move beyond the systems that created the metacrisis, we need a concurrent shift in the very ways we view, sense, relate to, and engage with the world — including what we value and how we understand our place in the web of life. This means centring multiple ways of knowing: Indigenous epistemologies, community knowledge, embodied experience, relational understanding.

**In practice:** The narrative *"Land Back to Right Relations"* illustrates what Justice + Onto-shift looks like together. "Land Back" names the anticolonial demand for redistribution of power and reparation (Justice). "Right Relations" carries the "all my relations" philosophy present in numerous Indigenous cultures — the interdependence of all beings, mutual exchange, relational duty (Onto-shift). Neither alone is sufficient. Together they open a different map of reality.

All agents in this system operate from this stance.

## The Method as a Cycle

The Culture Hack Method is **not a linear sequence**. It is an iterative cycle:

```
        ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        ↓                                               ↑
Ask → Listen → Understand → Recode → Hack + Evaluate
 01    02a→02b      03          04       05 + 06
```

On iteration: narrative practitioners update their Listening Model with new parameters rather than rebuilding it. Community profiles are refined, not rediscovered. The POV may be sharpened based on what the hack revealed. Each cycle deepens the practice.

**Evaluate is nested within Hack**, not a separate endpoint. It connects the tail of one hack to the opening of the next cycle. Baselines are set during Listen, narrative objectives defined during Recode, and measurement planned during Hack design.

## The Five Phases

### Ask — Point of View
Narrative practitioners develop a collective **Point of View (POV)** — who they are, what narrative problem they observe, what they want to change, and their theory of how that change happens.

**Format:** *"We are [collective identity] / we see that [narrative observation] / Therefore we want [vision] / To do this, we must [theory of change]"*

The POV names a narrative problem, not just a material condition. Two formats are produced: an Extended POV (full paragraph) and a Short POV (1–3 sentences) which becomes the direct input to the Listen phase.

*(If working within the Rhizome Fellowship, the POV connects to one of the Five Pathways to Regenerative Futures — see below.)*

---

### Listen — Narrative Research
The Short POV is transformed into a **research question** that orients all listening parameters.

Narrative practitioners design a **Listening Model** with five parameters: **What** (topics/keywords) · **Who** (actors/communities) · **When** (timeframe) · **Where** (platforms/geography) · **How** (method and documentation).

Two listening modes operate in parallel:
- **Small Listening:** Qualitative and relationship-based — interviews, surveys, manual media analysis. Texture, nuance, direct community voice.
- **Big Listening:** Large-scale digital research. Broader patterns, network dynamics, reach at scale.

The Listen phase produces a **Narrative Report** and **CSV Database** — the baseline all later analysis and impact evaluation will compare against.

---

### Understand — Narrative Analysis
Four analytical tools applied in sequence:

**ANP Analysis:** Attention (volume, reach, resonance) · Network (relationships, topology, bridges) · Power (institutional, cultural, network). Communities with the most attention and network interactions over time have the most power.

**Language Analysis:** Systematic frame and metaphor analysis using the three frame questions. Identifies dominant metaphors, ideological logic, and how common sense is constructed in each community.

**Justice/Ontology Mapping:** A two-axis map for positioning narrative communities:
- Justice axis (X): Reform/Individual ↔ Systemic/Transformative
- Ontology axis (Y): Singular ways of knowing ↔ Plural ways of knowing
- Four quadrants: Upper Right (Justice + Onto-shift) · Upper Left (Injustice + Ontofake) · Lower Right (Justice + Reform) · Lower Left (Injustice + Monoculture)

**Window of Discourse:** Mainstream/Acceptable ↔ Emerging/Contested ↔ Radical/Unacceptable. Reveals where to intervene to expand what is considered possible.

---

### Recode — Narrative Strategy
**Decode:** Map the hidden structure of a narrative — its verbs, subjects/objects, evoked images, hidden assumptions, truth claims, ideology, metaphors, and framing. Each element reveals a political choice embedded in language.

**Recode:** Build an alternative narrative guided by narrative objectives and the target audience. Set **Narrative Objectives** — which communities to engage, what direction of movement on the Justice/Ontology map, which frames to challenge, strengthen, or introduce. Translate into a concrete **Message**: exact language that activates the new frames. The message is not a slogan — it is the structure of meaning the hack builds on.

---

### Hack + Evaluate — Intervention and Learning
**Hack:** Translate the message into a cultural intervention through the **Hacking Tree**:
- **Media** — format and platform
- **Meme** — the message as a sticky cultural unit (symbol + association + synthesis)
- **Tone** — emotions evoked and spread
- **Hack** — Space, Scale, and Moment

Plus **Logistics**: timeline, team, capacities, budget, documentation, monitoring.

The hack must embody the recoded narrative, not simply communicate it.

**Evaluate:** Impact assessed at three levels:
- **Audience impact** — reach, response, adoption (weeks to months)
- **Discourse impact** — shifts in ANP and Window of Discourse position (months)
- **Systems change contribution** — contribution to longer-term tipping points (years)

Output: a **Case Study** that documents the full arc and feeds into the next cycle.

## Narrative Communities

Groups of actors who persistently engage with a specific topic using shared Actions & Expressions organised by common frames, metaphors, and ideological logic. The primary unit of analysis in Understand and of engagement in Recode and Hack.

**Three types:**
- **Hegemonic** — reinforce dominant constructs and existing power structures
- **Alternative** — resist or propose alternatives; exist at the fringes of mainstream
- **Catalytic** — high potential to drive systems-level narrative shift; already pushing in the desired direction
